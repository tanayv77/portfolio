About

From technical systems to usable products.

I studied Physics at the University of Groningen, then worked as a Data Engineer and Quantitative Research Intern at Syntagma Labs. Most of that work sat close to models, pipelines, and noisy inputs.

The part I kept coming back to was what happened after the system produced an answer. A good model can still be hard to use if the interface does not show the right signal, the right context, or the right amount of uncertainty.

That is the thread through these projects. I am most interested in products where system behavior has to become legible: a desktop diagnostic widget, a hand-controlled AR scene, a simulation, or a constrained recommendation flow.

Project 01

Pulse

A calm desktop utility built to answer whether the laptop is hot, constrained, draining battery unusually, or behaving differently from a few minutes ago.

Role

Product framing, interaction design, system-state modelling

Type

Windows desktop utility

Timeline

Ongoing product build

Status

Tauri 2 desktop app with persistent system history

The problem

When a laptop suddenly feels hot, slow, or battery-hungry, the user usually has a simple question: what changed? Existing tools are good at showing telemetry, but much worse at explaining whether performance is actually constrained or whether something unusual is happening.

I did not want to make a prettier Task Manager. The product had to be diagnosis-first, honest about missing telemetry, and calm enough to live on the desktop without feeling like a gamer overlay.

Who used it

The user is someone working on a Windows laptop who notices that the machine feels off. They are not starting with a performance graph in mind. They want to know whether the system is warm, actually constrained, or just briefly busy.

That means the first layer has to answer the question quickly, while deeper layers can hold the process detail and recent history for someone who wants to inspect further.

My role and process

I framed Pulse around three layers: a compact widget, an expanded panel, and a secondary details window. The design work was deciding what each layer should answer, and what should stay out of the first glance.

Underneath that, the product uses a Rust backend to own the system logic, a low-overhead sampling engine, and a heuristic state model for battery and thermal pressure. The React and TypeScript frontend renders backend-authored view state, which keeps the interface clean and stops the widget from becoming a second source of truth.

Key design decisions

  • Keep the widget small and diagnostic. The compact view only shows core state, CPU, RAM, and temperature when supported. Offender processes and richer explanation are intentionally pushed into deeper layers.
  • Use state, not charts, in the first layer. The widget avoids tiny graphs because they look informative while often being harder to read than a direct system state. A single clear label is more useful when the user is checking quickly.
  • Handle partial telemetry honestly. Thermal data is not always available. Instead of faking certainty or showing an error state, Pulse reduces the visible metrics and explains what it can still infer.

What the user sees

The first thing the user sees is a compact widget with a single state label and a short explanation: healthy, warm, hot, thermally constrained, or critical. Below that sit CPU and RAM, plus temperature when the system exposes it.

Opening the expanded panel adds recent change, battery pressure, and plain-English interpretation of the current state. A secondary details window holds process-level information, tray controls, pause and resume, and the stored history in SQLite so the product can answer not just what is happening now, but what changed recently.

How the information is layered

1

Compact widget

Shows the current system state and only the minimum metrics needed to judge whether the machine feels normal.

2

Expanded panel

Adds explanation, pressure cues, and recent change so the user can tell whether the problem is building or settling.

3

Detail layer

Process information, history, and tray actions stay one layer deeper for people who need to investigate without burdening every user.

Outcome

The strongest shift was moving away from telemetry display and toward system diagnosis. Once the product was framed around the question the user actually has, the interface became more restrained and much easier to trust.

What I’d change

The current heuristics still rely on fixed thresholds. The next step would be learning baselines from the individual machine so Pulse can tell the difference between normal sustained load and behavior that is genuinely unusual for that laptop.

The widget only works if it answers what changed without forcing the user to read a dashboard.

Project 02

AirCoaster

A native Android AR interaction study focused on making placement, movement, and resizing feel stable enough to believe.

Role

Interaction design, product direction, prototyping

Type

Native Android AR app

Timeline

Independent build

Status

ARCore and hand tracking working together in a native flow

The problem

The original browser-based approach could prove the idea, but it was not good enough to make the interaction believable. Drift, lag, false placements, and unstable pinch detection all made the product feel like a demo instead of a tool.

The design problem was not just getting an object into a room. It was making the relationship between user intention and system response feel tight enough that people would trust what they were seeing.

Who used it

The user is holding up a phone, looking through the rear camera, and trying to place or adjust a 3D object directly in space. They are making quick judgement calls with their hands and their eyes, so every unstable response immediately feels wrong.

That makes AR interaction unusually unforgiving. Small delays, bad gesture reads, or objects slipping across the plane are not minor bugs. They break the whole experience.

My role and process

I changed the product direction once it became clear that the browser implementation was too limited. Moving to native Android let me use ARCore more directly, share the camera with MediaPipe-based hand tracking, and treat the interaction as a real-time product problem instead of a technical demo.

From there I focused on the interaction states: when an object is only a preview, when a pinch should count as intent, and how movement or resizing should hold steady instead of reacting to every noisy frame.

Key design decisions

  • Preview before placement. New objects first appear as a tentative preview, which gives the user a chance to judge the plane before anything is committed to the scene.
  • Treat gestures as interaction states. Place, move, and resize are separate states. The app does not treat every detected pinch as the same command, which reduces accidental jumps and false placements.
  • Be honest about unstable tracking. When the scene or hand signal is weak, the object should hold position rather than pretending the transform is reliable. Stability matters more than looking fast.

What the user sees

The main screen is the rear camera feed with a light UI around it. The object enters as a placement preview, then becomes a real scene object once the user commits it and tracking is stable enough to hold it in place.

After placement, the user can move or resize the object through hand-based gestures. The interface keeps the status cues small: enough to tell the user whether the app is ready, tracking, or waiting, without covering the scene with controls.

How the interaction holds together

1

Read the scene

ARCore establishes the plane and the app shows a preview object instead of committing immediately.

2

Capture intent

Hand tracking turns pinch input into a controlled state so the object responds to intention, not every frame.

3

Hold stability

Once placed, the object should feel anchored. When tracking weakens, the system favors steadiness over aggressive motion.

Outcome

The move to native changed the project from a proof of concept into a real interaction design problem. It became much clearer how much of AR credibility comes from state handling, gesture thresholds, and restraint rather than visual spectacle.

What I’d change

The next step would be better recovery when tracking degrades. Right now I understand why an object might pause or hold, but a new user needs clearer guidance about what the system is waiting for and how to get back to a stable state.

In AR, a slightly wrong response feels more fake than a slightly delayed one.

Project 03

Physics Engine

An interactive simulation project about turning abstract rules into motion, collision, and response that people can actually read.

Role

Simulation design, interaction framing, behavior tuning

Type

Interactive simulation

Timeline

Independent build

Status

Playable simulation with direct manipulation

The problem

A physics engine can be mathematically interesting and still be hard to understand from the outside. If motion looks unstable, collisions feel arbitrary, or controls are too loose, the user stops reading the scene as a coherent system.

The product question was how to make simulated behavior legible. I was not just interested in getting bodies to move. I wanted the user to be able to disturb the system and understand why it responded the way it did.

Who used it

The user here is someone exploring behavior through interaction. They might be testing a scene, dragging an object, or comparing two responses after changing an initial condition.

That means the simulation needs to feel believable before it feels impressive. Stability, readable motion, and clear control matter more than exposing every possible parameter.

My role and process

I treated the engine as an interface problem as much as an implementation problem. The work sat in choosing how much of the underlying system to expose, how to tune motion so it felt responsive without becoming chaotic, and how to keep the main simulation stage readable.

That led to a simple structure: a central stage, a small control bar for play, pause, and reset, and direct interaction with the bodies inside the scene. The engine exists underneath, but the design question is whether the resulting behavior feels legible to a person watching it.

Key design decisions

  • Favor believable behavior over maximum responsiveness. A slightly slower but stable response is easier to trust than motion that reacts instantly but jitters, tunnels, or explodes under collision.
  • Keep the control surface small. The interface exposes the handful of actions that support understanding the scene, rather than turning the product into a panel of solver parameters.
  • Make interaction teach the rules. Dragging, releasing, colliding, and resetting are part of the learning loop. The user should understand the behavior by disturbing the system and watching the response.

What the user sees

The main view is a simulation stage with bodies, constraints, and collisions visible through motion rather than debug text. A small control bar lets the user start, pause, reset, and switch between scene states without pulling focus away from the stage.

When the user interacts with the scene, the system needs to make the result readable: bodies should settle when expected, impacts should feel proportional, and constraint relationships should be visible through how the objects move together.

How the simulation is experienced

1

Set the scene

A simple control layer establishes the initial state without crowding the interface with technical tuning.

2

Disturb the system

The user drags, releases, or collides objects to see how the system responds under changed conditions.

3

Read the response

The resulting motion needs to communicate cause and effect, not just prove that the solver is running.

Outcome

The value of the project came from treating simulated behavior as an experience to be shaped. Once the goal became legibility rather than feature count, decisions about controls, motion, and scene design became much clearer.

What I’d change

I would add better comparison tools between runs. Right now the user can feel that one initial condition behaves differently from another, but a stronger product would make those differences easier to inspect without cluttering the stage.

The simulation becomes useful when people can see cause and effect, not just motion.

Project 04

MedBot

A safer health guidance flow built around intake first, recommendation second, and structured outputs instead of a wall of conversational text.

Role

Conversation design, safety framing, structured output design

Type

Guided health recommendation product

Timeline

Independent concept

Status

Working flow with safety filtering and recommendation cards

The problem

Health information online often fails in one of two ways: it is generic enough to be useless, or specific enough to sound overconfident. The design challenge here was to make guidance feel usable without acting as if the product could skip context.

I did not want a freeform chatbot that guessed its way into a recommendation. The product needed to collect the right safety constraints first and make them visible enough that the user understood why certain advice appeared and other advice did not.

Who used it

The user is someone dealing with symptoms, managing a condition, or trying to make a health-related decision without medical training. They may be tired, worried, or short on attention.

That makes flow design central. The product has to reduce overload, ask only for the information it needs, and avoid making the user read a long cautionary paragraph before anything useful appears.

My role and process

I framed this as a guided product flow rather than a generic conversational interface. The core design work was mapping the intake sequence, the contraindication logic, and the output structure so the experience stayed useful and constrained at the same time.

The flow became four steps: choose a profile, capture relevant conditions, apply safety filtering, then show structured recommendation cards. That ordering keeps the product from offering anything before it has enough context to justify it.

How the flow works

Step 1

Profile selection

The user chooses a context such as general wellness or managing a condition before the system starts reasoning.

Step 2

Condition capture

Contraindications such as high blood pressure are collected and become active filters behind the scenes.

Step 3

Safety filtering

Structured recommendation logic removes advice that does not fit the profile instead of showing it and correcting later.

Step 4

Structured output

The user sees cards with what to do, why it applies, and what would make it inappropriate.

Key design decisions

  • Do not recommend before context exists. Profile and contraindication capture come first, which keeps the product from making premature suggestions and gives the guidance a clear basis.
  • Make safe mode visible but quiet. The interface shows that safety filtering is active, but it does not turn the whole product into a warning label.
  • Keep the output structured. Recommendation cards are easier to scan than a paragraph. They also give the product space to show why the guidance applies and when it should not be used.

What the user sees

The experience starts with a simple intake, not an empty text box. Once the user has chosen a profile and flagged relevant conditions, the product shows filtered recommendation cards with a short explanation and a small note about when not to use them.

The visible interface stays light: a small safe-mode badge, clear section labels, and detail on request rather than all at once. The aim is to make the product feel supportive without pretending it can safely answer everything in one sentence.

Outcome

Treating the product as a guided flow made it much more credible. The design stopped sounding like a chatbot pitch and started behaving like a constrained recommendation product with a clear safety model.

What I’d change

The interaction is still mostly linear. A stronger version would let people add context halfway through, revise earlier answers, and move back through the intake without feeling like they are restarting the whole conversation.

Guidance becomes more trustworthy when the user can see both why it fits and what might rule it out.

Reflection

How I work through design decisions.

1

Show the right thing, not everything

On the risk product, that meant putting probability, confidence, and trend ahead of raw inputs. On the blockchain tool, it meant moving noisy transaction logs out of the main view without deleting them.

2

Trust is earned through honesty about uncertainty

I try to show where the product might be wrong instead of hiding it. That is why the depeg tool shows confidence next to the score, and why the health tool pairs each recommendation with a note on when not to use it.

3

Constraints are better than blank canvases

The strongest design decisions in these projects came from hard limits: noisy market signals, safety rules, and messy wallet activity. Constraints made the interface more specific, not less ambitious.
